# Sweep the Floor

Source: Dave Hoover and Adewale Oshineye, *Apprenticeship Patterns* (2009) — provenance: author-verified

## When it applies

- Joining a new team and looking for a first meaningful contribution
- Tempted to claim visible or architectural work before trust is earned
- Grunt work is being avoided as beneath the session

## The rule

On a new team, find the work nobody wants — flaky tests, doc gaps, dependency upgrades, build tooling — and do it well; the unglamorous corners teach the system's seams faster than the architecture diagrams. It's an entry move, not a residence: graduate to harder work once trust is earned, hand the floor to the next new person, and keep the willingness even when senior.

## Anti-patterns

- **Architect-on-arrival** — demanding the interesting work before earning trust; good ideas nobody will hear yet
- **Maintenance-as-beneath-me** — routine work as a status statement
- **Sweeping forever** — "humble" as a hiding place from stretch
- **Performative sweeping** — scut work for visible credit rather than to learn

## In the agentic era

Agents make the visible work cheap to produce, which makes **Architect-on-arrival** more tempting than ever — a newcomer can generate a redesign proposal in an hour without knowing where the bodies are buried. When someone early on a team reaches for the visible work, surface whether a concrete unglamorous task would earn trust faster while teaching the codebase; when they signal onboarding ambiguity ("what should I work on?"), offer floor-sweeping candidates from context. Pragmatic, never moralistic.
