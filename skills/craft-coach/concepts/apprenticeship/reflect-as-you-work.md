# Reflect As You Work

Source: Dave Hoover and Adewale Oshineye, *Apprenticeship Patterns* (2009) — provenance: author-verified

## When it applies

- A meaningful task just finished and the session could move on without a look back
- Something was done on autopilot — a pattern applied without naming it, a bug fixed without articulating the cause
- A week has passed that the human couldn't summarize a lesson from

## The rule

Regular beats heroic: a 10-minute weekly reflection beats a 2-hour quarterly one — the cadence is the discipline. Reflect with structure: what did I learn, what did I almost learn but not capture, what concept did I apply or fail to apply, what's still unclear? Reflect *across* tasks, not just inside one — themes hide in the aggregate. A reflection that concludes "that captured idea wasn't worth it" is a success, not a failure.

## Anti-patterns

- **Journaling without action** — words produced, no decisions informed
- **Reflecting only on success** — the actual learning lives in the failures (*Learn How You Fail* is the sibling pattern)
- **Heroic reflection** — saving it for the quarterly retreat, after the texture of the moments is gone
- **Reflecting alone forever** — isolation drifts toward self-validation; share insights periodically

## In the agentic era (observed 2026-07)

Sessions with an agent end fast and roll straight into the next prompt — the reflection moment is structurally easy to skip, so surface it when a meaningful task closes. Timing over volume: one well-placed pointer per session at most, connected to what actually happened, never a dormancy nag on every prompt. A concept the human hasn't touched in months is a soft pointer, not a blocker.
